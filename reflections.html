Project Reflections

Honest insights into our learning journey, challenges overcome, and lessons learned for future projects

What We Learned About Teamwork

Communication is Everything

Clear, frequent communication prevented misunderstandings and kept everyone aligned on goals. Regular check-ins and transparent sharing of challenges can lead to better outcomes than trying to solve problems in isolation.

Collaboration is critical

This project Highlighted to us just how important collaboration is in a large project. Each Member had a part to play and the projects success strongly depended on the six of us working together and giving it our all

Iteration Requires Trust

Willingness to receive feedback and iterate on ideas required building trust within the team. Creating a safe environment for constructive criticism led to dramatically better outcomes than our initial individual efforts.

It’s Okay to Let Go

We learned that not every idea will fit the project's needs, no matter how much effort went into it. Being able to let go of unsuitable concepts allowed us to focus on stronger, more viable directions. Scrapping ideas isn’t failure — it’s part of progress.

Team Reflection Essay

What We're Proud Of

Looking back at this short three week project, our team is proud of the solution we managed to come up with. Tackling the complex problem statement provided is not easy. However with rapid iteration, and a willingness to learn and adapt, we were able to navigate the challenges and arrive at a solution that we believe is both practical and meaningful. The tight timeline forced us to stay focused and make decisions quickly, but it also taught us the importance of prioritising and trusting each other’s strengths.

We are also proud of how the design process was handled. Rather than stubbornly clinging to the initial idea, we showed willingness to scrap work already completed and completely shift the targeted audience for the project which showed professional maturity and a commitment to excellence.

While the project had its fair share of challenges, we’re proud that we stayed committed to seeing it through to completion. Even when motivation dipped or things felt uncertain, our team continued to show up, contribute, and learn from our mistakes and that attitude showed is something we are proud of.

What We're Not Proud Of

Our initial proposed solution had too broad a scope. We tried to incorporate both public education on the dangers of Artificial Lights at Night (ALAN) while also providing the data collection and visualization needed by our main stakeholder. In doing so, our solution suffered as a result — lacking focus, it did not effectively meet either goal.

After careful discussion, we made the difficult decision to narrow our scope, scrap the prototype we had developed, and refocus our solution toward the core need of our main stakeholder: consistent and reliable data collection.

While our project met its minimum requirements, we recognise that our team could have grown more in terms of collaboration and communication. At times, we struggled with unclear delegation, uneven participation, and unspoken assumptions about responsibilities. These issues led to delays, confusion, and moments of frustration. We placed too much emphasis on "just getting things done" and not enough on building a shared understanding. This reminded us that soft skills such as empathy, active listening, and accountability are just as critical as technical ability.

While our project managed to achieve the goal in the end on the technical side, more could be done in our collaborative side. Strengthening our team dynamics, building trust earlier, and fostering a more open and supportive environment could have improved not just the process, but also the overall experience for everyone involved. This project reminded us that successful outcomes aren’t just about functionality but are also about how well we work together to get there.

What We Would Do Differently

If we could restart this project, the first change would be to define a clearer and more focused scope from the beginning. Our initial ambition to tackle both public education and stakeholder data needs stretched us too thin. With better prioritisation early on, we could have avoided wasting time on features that were ultimately scrapped.

We also would have established stronger communication habits within the team. Unclear delegation and unspoken assumptions often led to misunderstandings and uneven workloads. Setting clear expectations, defining roles early, and having structured check-ins would have improved collaboration and team morale.
