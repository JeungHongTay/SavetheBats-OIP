Project Reflections

Honest insights into our learning journey, challenges overcome, and lessons for future projects

Interactions with Partners & Stakeholders

Throughout our project, we engaged with multiple stakeholders who provided invaluable insights that shaped our approach and final solution. These interactions were crucial in ensuring our solution addressed real-world needs and constraints.

Glasgow City Council Environmental Team

What We Learned:

  • Policy Constraints: Understanding the bureaucratic process for implementing new monitoring systems and the need for evidence-based decision making
  • Budget Realities: Learning about tight municipal budgets and the importance of cost-effective solutions that provide clear ROI
  • Data Requirements: The council needs specific data formats and reporting mechanisms for compliance with environmental regulations
  • Long-term Sustainability: Solutions must be maintainable with existing staff and technical capabilities

Feedback That Changed Our Approach:

"We love the real-time monitoring concept, but we need to ensure the data is actionable. Can you show us exactly how this helps us make lighting policy decisions?" — Senior Environmental Officer (Week 3 interview)

This feedback led us to add specific policy recommendation features and clearer correlation visualizations between light levels and bat activity.

Glasgow Bat Group & Conservation Experts

What We Learned:

  • Scientific Accuracy: The importance of using correct terminology and avoiding oversimplification of complex ecological relationships
  • Seasonal Variations: Bat behavior changes dramatically with seasons, breeding cycles, and weather patterns
  • Species Differences: Different bat species have varying sensitivity to light pollution and require different monitoring approaches
  • Community Education: Public engagement is crucial for long-term conservation success

Critical Feedback:

"Your initial design focused too much on generic 'bat activity.' We need to distinguish between species and understand feeding vs. commuting behavior patterns." — Lead Researcher, Glasgow Bat Group (Week 4 consultation)

This expert input led us to refine our data categorization and add species-specific tracking to the adopt-a-bat program.

Community Representatives & Park Users

What We Learned:

  • Accessibility Needs: Many community members are not tech-savvy and need simple, intuitive interfaces
  • Personal Connection: People engage more when they can connect emotionally with individual animals
  • Educational Hunger: Strong interest in learning about local wildlife when information is presented accessibly
  • Practical Concerns: Questions about safety and park lighting for human users

Valuable Insights:

"I had no idea bats lived in our local park! If I could follow a specific bat and see how the lights affect it, that would make me care so much more about this issue." — Local Park User (Week 5 user testing)

This insight reinforced our decision to implement the adopt-a-bat feature as a core engagement mechanism.

Key Changes from Partner Feedback

Data Presentation

Added policy-focused views and actionable insights specifically for council decision-making

Scientific Accuracy

Implemented species-specific tracking and more nuanced behavioral categorization

Community Features

Enhanced the adopt-a-bat program with educational content and personal tracking

Accessibility

Simplified language, added tooltips, and improved mobile experience

Personal Reflection Essay

What I'm Proud Of

Looking back on this eight-week journey, I'm most proud of how our team maintained a user-centered approach throughout the entire development process. From the beginning, we committed to solving a real problem for real people, and I believe we succeeded in creating something genuinely useful.

The technical implementation exceeded my expectations. Building a fully functional dashboard with interactive charts, responsive design, and engaging user features taught me that ambitious projects are achievable with proper planning and iterative development. The adopt-a-bat feature, in particular, demonstrates how technology can create emotional connections that drive behavioral change.

I'm also proud of how we handled stakeholder feedback. Rather than seeing criticism as setbacks, we embraced each piece of feedback as an opportunity to improve. The willingness to completely redesign features based on user testing shows professional maturity and commitment to excellence.

What I'm Not Proud Of

Our initial research phase was too narrow. We focused heavily on technical solutions without adequately understanding the political and budgetary constraints facing our primary users. This led to early concepts that, while technically impressive, weren't practically implementable by Glasgow City Council with their current resources.

I also regret not involving accessibility experts earlier in the design process. While we made accessibility improvements in later weeks, incorporating inclusive design principles from the beginning would have resulted in a better product for all users. The late addition of accessibility features felt reactive rather than intentional.

Time management was another area where we could have performed better. We spent too much time in the early ideation phase exploring features that ultimately didn't make it into the final product. A more structured approach to feature prioritization would have allowed us to spend more time polishing the core functionality.

Key Challenges and How We Addressed Them

Challenge 1: Balancing Simplicity with Scientific Accuracy

The Problem: Conservation experts wanted detailed, scientifically accurate data presentation, while community users needed simple, accessible information. These seemed like conflicting requirements.

Our Solution: We implemented a layered information architecture with summary views for casual users and detailed drill-down capabilities for experts. Tooltips and progressive disclosure allowed us to maintain simplicity on the surface while providing depth when needed.

Lesson Learned: Complex user requirements don't always require complex solutions. Good UX design can accommodate different user needs within the same interface.

Challenge 2: Creating Emotional Connection with Data

The Problem: Environmental data can feel abstract and impersonal. We needed to make bat conservation feel personally relevant to community members who might never have seen a bat.

Our Solution: The adopt-a-bat program transforms abstract conservation data into personal stories. By following individual bats and seeing how lighting affects their specific behaviors, users develop emotional investment in conservation outcomes.

Lesson Learned: Storytelling and personalization are powerful tools for engaging users with complex social and environmental issues.

Challenge 3: Technical Complexity vs. Performance

The Problem: Our initial dashboard designs included complex real-time visualizations that caused performance issues, especially on mobile devices.

Our Solution: We implemented progressive enhancement, starting with fast-loading basic charts and adding interactivity for users with capable devices. We also optimized our code and used efficient chart libraries.

Lesson Learned: Performance is a feature. Slow applications frustrate users regardless of how impressive the functionality might be.

What I Would Do Differently

If I could restart this project, I would begin with a much more structured stakeholder analysis. Understanding the political, technical, and budgetary constraints of each stakeholder group should have been our first step, not something we discovered during development.

I would also implement a more rigorous testing schedule from the beginning. While our final user testing was valuable, earlier and more frequent testing would have prevented us from pursuing design directions that ultimately didn't work for our users.

Technical architecture decisions should have been made more deliberately. We chose technologies based on familiarity rather than optimal fit for the project requirements. A more thorough technical evaluation might have led to better performance and easier maintenance.

Finally, I would have documented our design decisions better throughout the process. While our weekly journal captures major milestones, we lost many valuable insights about why specific design choices were made and rejected.

Impact and Future Potential

While this project was developed as an academic exercise, the positive response from Glasgow City Council suggests genuine potential for real-world implementation. The councilors we interviewed expressed interest in piloting aspects of our solution, particularly the sensor network and policy dashboard features.

More importantly, this project has changed how I think about the relationship between technology and environmental conservation. Technology alone cannot solve environmental problems, but well-designed digital tools can facilitate the human connections and behavioral changes that drive real conservation outcomes.

The skills I've developed in user research, stakeholder management, and iterative design will be valuable in any future project. Learning to balance competing requirements and make evidence-based design decisions has prepared me for the complex challenges of professional product development.

Looking Forward

Potential Real-World Impact

Based on stakeholder feedback, there's genuine interest in implementing aspects of this solution in Glasgow. The city council has indicated they would consider a pilot program if funding becomes available, particularly for the sensor network component.

Scalability Opportunities

The principles and approach we developed could be adapted for other cities facing similar wildlife conservation challenges. The modular design would allow implementation of individual components (sensors, dashboard, community features) based on local needs and resources.

Technical Evolution

Future versions could incorporate machine learning for pattern recognition, IoT sensor integration for real-time data collection, and mobile applications for field researchers. The foundation we've built provides a strong platform for these enhancements.

Lessons for Future Projects

  • Start with stakeholder constraints, not technical possibilities
  • Invest in user research early and often
  • Design for performance and accessibility from the beginning
  • Document decision-making processes for future reference
  • Build in flexibility for evolving requirements
