Project Reflections

Honest insights into our 3-week learning journey, challenges overcome, and lessons learned for future projects

What We Learned About Teamwork

Communication is Everything

The tight 3-week timeline forced us to develop clear, frequent communication patterns. Regular check-ins during our scheduled tutor discussions and partner meetings prevented misunderstandings and kept everyone aligned on goals. We learned that transparent sharing of challenges leads to better outcomes than trying to solve problems in isolation.

Collaboration is Critical

This project highlighted just how important collaboration is in a large project. Each member had a specific role to play, and the project's success strongly depended on the six of us working together and giving it our all. The partner meetings and group sessions showed us that diverse perspectives lead to stronger solutions.

Iteration Requires Trust

The prototyping and building sessions taught us that willingness to receive feedback and iterate on ideas required building trust within the team. Creating a safe environment for constructive criticism during our tutor discussions led to dramatically better outcomes than our initial individual efforts.

It's Okay to Let Go

We learned that not every idea will fit the project's needs, no matter how much effort went into it. Being able to let go of unsuitable concepts during our development phases allowed us to focus on stronger, more viable directions. Scrapping ideas isn't failure — it's part of progress.

Team Reflection Essay

What We're Proud Of

Looking back at this intensive three-week project, our team is proud of the solution we managed to develop despite the challenging timeline. Tackling the complex problem of bat conservation and light pollution in Glasgow required rapid iteration and a willingness to learn and adapt. We successfully navigated the challenges and arrived at a solution that we believe is both practical and meaningful.

We are particularly proud of how we handled the design process throughout the three weeks. From the initial intro sessions and contract exercises in Week 1, through the usability testing and partner meetings in Week 2, to the final exhibition preparation in Week 3, we showed professional maturity and a commitment to excellence. Rather than stubbornly clinging to initial ideas, we demonstrated willingness to iterate and adapt based on feedback from tutors and stakeholders.

The tight timeline forced us to stay focused and make decisions quickly, but it also taught us the importance of prioritizing and trusting each other's strengths. We're proud that we stayed committed to seeing the project through to completion, even when motivation dipped or things felt uncertain. Our team continued to show up, contribute, and learn from our mistakes throughout all three weeks.

What We're Not Proud Of

Our initial proposed solution had too broad a scope. We tried to incorporate both public education on the dangers of Artificial Lights at Night (ALAN) while also providing the data collection and visualization needed by our main stakeholder. In doing so, our solution suffered as a result — lacking focus, it did not effectively meet either goal.

After careful discussion during our Week 2 partner meetings and tutor sessions, we made the difficult decision to narrow our scope, scrap the prototype we had developed, and refocus our solution toward the core need of our main stakeholder: consistent and reliable data collection.

While our project met its minimum requirements, we recognize that our team could have grown more in terms of collaboration and communication. At times, we struggled with unclear delegation, uneven participation, and unspoken assumptions about responsibilities. These issues led to delays, confusion, and moments of frustration during our development sessions. We placed too much emphasis on "just getting things done" and not enough on building a shared understanding.

The project reminded us that soft skills such as empathy, active listening, and accountability are just as critical as technical ability. While our project managed to achieve the goal in the end on the technical side, more could be done in our collaborative side. Strengthening our team dynamics, building trust earlier, and fostering a more open and supportive environment could have improved not just the process, but also the overall experience for everyone involved.

What We Would Do Differently

If we could restart this project, the first change would be to define a clearer and more focused scope from the beginning. Our initial ambition to tackle both public education and stakeholder data needs stretched us too thin. With better prioritization early on, we could have avoided wasting time on features that were ultimately scrapped during our Week 2 pivot.

We also would have established stronger communication habits within the team from Week 1. Unclear delegation and unspoken assumptions often led to misunderstandings and uneven workloads during our development sessions. Setting clear expectations, defining roles early, and having more structured check-ins would have improved collaboration and team morale throughout the three weeks.

Additionally, we would have taken better advantage of the scheduled tutor discussions and feedback sessions. While we attended these sessions, we could have been more proactive in seeking guidance and feedback earlier in the process, rather than waiting until we encountered problems.

Key Learnings from Our 3-Week Journey

Time Management

The tight timeline taught us the importance of effective time management and prioritization. We learned to focus on what truly matters and let go of perfectionism.

Stakeholder Engagement

Regular partner meetings and stakeholder interviews showed us the value of understanding user needs early and often in the design process.

Iterative Design

The prototyping sessions and usability testing taught us that good design comes from iteration and user feedback, not from perfect first attempts.

Team Dynamics

Working in a team of six over three weeks revealed the importance of clear communication, trust, and shared goals in project success.
