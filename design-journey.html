Stage 1: Research & Discovery

Deep dive into bat conservation challenges and stakeholder needs

‚è± Duration: 1 Day

üîç Objectives:

  • Understand the ecological and political challenges behind bat conservation
  • Identify user pain points (volunteers, council, public)
  • Investigate how light pollution affects bat activity
  • Evaluate existing monitoring efforts and their limitations

ü§î Strategic Reasoning Behind Our Research Approach:

Why Literature Review First?

Evidence-Based Foundation: We prioritized academic research over assumptions because conservation work demands scientific credibility. Without understanding the actual mechanisms of light pollution impact, our solution could miss critical factors or oversimplify complex ecological relationships.

Stakeholder Credibility: Council officials and researchers would only trust recommendations backed by peer-reviewed evidence, not just observational claims.

Why Focus on Multiple Stakeholder Perspectives?

System Thinking: Bat conservation isn't just about bats‚Äîit involves volunteers (data collectors), councils (policy makers), researchers (experts), and the public (behavior change). Each group has different mental models of what constitutes "proof" and "action."

Design for Adoption: A tool that only serves researchers won't create policy change; a tool that only engages the public won't generate scientific insight. We needed to understand how these groups intersect.

Why Shared Mental Model Mapping?

Avoiding Design Assumptions: Rather than assuming what stakeholders needed, we mapped their existing understanding and expectations. This revealed that "evidence" means different things to different groups‚Äîdetailed graphs for researchers, simple alerts for volunteers, impact summaries for council.

Interface Strategy: This technique helped us design a dashboard that could serve multiple mental models through different views rather than forcing one approach on all users.

Stage 2: Ideation & Lo-fi Prototyping

From brainstorming innovative solutions to rapid paper prototypes

‚è± Duration: 2 Days

üß≠ Objectives:

  • Translate stakeholder needs into actionable ideas
  • Brainstorm interactive features and interface concepts
  • Create rapid prototypes to validate core concepts
  • Test user flows and information architecture

üéØ Strategic Reasoning Behind Our Ideation Approach:

Why Mind Mapping Before Technical Analysis?

Divergent Before Convergent: We started with open exploration to avoid prematurely limiting our solution space. Mind mapping helped us identify unexpected connections between conservation, technology, policy, and community engagement that linear thinking might miss.

Stakeholder Integration: By mapping all stakeholder branches simultaneously, we could see where their needs intersected and diverged, informing our multi-user interface strategy.

Why Technical Complexity vs Impact Analysis?

Resource Realism: As students with limited time and technical resources, we needed a systematic way to prioritize ideas that would deliver maximum conservation impact within our constraints.

Strategic Communication: This framework helped us articulate to stakeholders why we focused on real-time dashboards over AR experiences‚Äînot because AR isn't valuable, but because dashboard solutions could be delivered effectively within our timeline while still creating meaningful change.

Future Planning: By plotting high-impact, high-complexity ideas, we created a roadmap for future development phases rather than simply discarding good concepts.

Why Paper Prototyping in a Digital Project?

Speed of Iteration: Hand-drawn sketches allowed us to rapidly test layout concepts, user flows, and information architecture without getting distracted by visual design details or technical constraints.

Stakeholder Communication: Low-fidelity sketches encouraged stakeholders to focus on functionality and content rather than debating colors or fonts, leading to more substantive feedback on core concepts.

Cost of Change: Discovering navigation issues or information hierarchy problems in sketches costs minutes; discovering them in coded prototypes costs hours or days.

Stage 3: Site Reconnaissance

On-site feasibility assessment and real-world constraints identification

‚è± Duration: 1 Day

üéØ Objectives:

  • Assess optimal sensor placement locations for bat monitoring
  • Evaluate field of view (FOV) constraints and coverage areas
  • Identify physical obstacles that could block sensor effectiveness
  • Determine best capture spots for comprehensive bat activity monitoring

ü§î Strategic Reasoning Behind Our Site Reconnaissance:

Why Conduct On-Site Assessment Before Design?

Reality Check: Paper prototypes and theoretical solutions needed validation against actual site conditions. Physical constraints like lighting infrastructure, visitor patterns, and environmental factors could significantly impact our design assumptions.

Stakeholder Context: Understanding how volunteers might move through the space, where they conduct monitoring, and what tools they currently use provided crucial insights that remote research couldn't capture.

Why Focus on Sensor Placement and FOV Assessment?

Coverage Optimization: Understanding the physical constraints of sensor placement was crucial to ensure comprehensive bat monitoring. Poor placement could result in blind spots or ineffective data collection, undermining the entire monitoring system's value.

Technical Feasibility: Field of view limitations and physical obstacles needed to be identified early to inform both our dashboard design and future sensor deployment strategies. What looks optimal on paper might be impractical in reality.

Why Document Capture Constraints?

Real-World Validation: The environmental context provides critical insights on optimal sensor setup for maximum effectiveness. A significant issue discovered during the site visit was realizing that field-of-view could be severely limited by obstacles if sensors are placed incorrectly.

Strategic Planning: Understanding where sensors can and cannot be effectively placed helps prioritize monitoring zones and informs the dashboard's data visualization strategy‚Äîshowing users which areas have reliable coverage versus areas with potential blind spots.

üîç Site Assessment Activities:

Sensor Placement Evaluation:

Systematic assessment of potential monitoring locations:

  • Coverage Analysis: Identified spots that could capture maximum bat activity across different flight paths
  • Field of View Testing: Measured and evaluated sensor FOV from various positions to ensure comprehensive monitoring
  • Obstacle Mapping: Documented trees, buildings, and structures that could block sensor effectiveness
  • Distance Assessment: Determined optimal sensor distances to avoid capture limitations due to range constraints

Physical Constraint Documentation:

Detailed evaluation of environmental factors affecting sensor deployment:

  • Mapped areas with clear lines of sight versus obstructed zones
  • Identified vegetation that could seasonally impact sensor effectiveness
  • Assessed how tree canopy density affects monitoring coverage
  • Evaluated ground-level versus elevated placement options for different areas

Capture Zone Optimization:

Strategic analysis of monitoring effectiveness across the site:

  • Best Capture Spots: Identified prime locations with unobstructed views and maximum bat activity potential
  • Problem Areas: Documented zones where obstacles or distance limitations would compromise data quality
  • Coverage Gaps: Mapped areas that would require multiple sensors or alternative monitoring approaches
  • Accessibility Balance: Found locations that optimize both capture effectiveness and practical sensor maintenance

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Implementation Challenges Identified:

Field of View Limitations: Sensors placed too far from bat activity areas would miss critical monitoring data
Tree Obstruction: Dense vegetation and tree branches could block sensor coverage of key flight paths
Optimal Positioning: Need to balance comprehensive bat capture with avoiding physical obstacles
Coverage Gaps: Some prime bat activity areas have limited sensor placement options due to obstructions
Distance Constraints: Sensor effectiveness decreases with distance, requiring strategic proximity planning
Multi-Angle Requirements: Single sensor positions may miss bats due to blind spots created by environmental features

üí° Design Insights & Adaptations:

Sensor Coverage Understanding

Site assessment provided valuable insights into the complexity of sensor placement and coverage challenges that would be important considerations for any future real-world sensor deployment in similar environments.

Physical Environment Constraints

Understanding the real-world obstacles and distance limitations helped validate the importance of comprehensive site assessment in conservation monitoring projects, even though these specific findings were not implemented in our current dashboard prototype.

Future Implementation Planning

The reconnaissance provided foundational knowledge about optimal capture locations that could inform future development phases, should the project progress to actual sensor deployment and real-time monitoring implementation.

üîÑ Impact on Hi-Fi Design Phase:

The reconnaissance provided valuable learning about the complexities of real-world sensor deployment:

  • Environmental Awareness: Gained understanding of the physical challenges that would need to be considered in future sensor deployment projects
  • Site Knowledge: Developed familiarity with Glasgow Botanic Garden's layout and constraints for potential future implementation
  • Practical Constraints: Better understanding of the gap between theoretical monitoring solutions and practical deployment challenges
  • Future Planning: Insights that could inform subsequent project phases should the dashboard progress to actual sensor integration

Stage 4: Hi-fi Design

Detailed Figma prototypes with visual design and interactions

‚è± Duration: 5 Days

üß† Strategic Reasoning Behind Our Hi-fi Design Approach:

Why Create a Design System First?

Consistency at Scale: Even though we were building a single application, establishing consistent colors, typography, and component patterns from the start ensured that different dashboard sections would feel unified rather than like separate tools.

Stakeholder Trust: Professional, consistent design signals credibility to council officials and researchers who need to trust our data presentations. Inconsistent visual design can undermine confidence in the underlying data.

Development Efficiency: Pre-defining visual patterns meant our development phase could focus on functionality rather than making ad-hoc design decisions.

Why Focus on Data Visualization Design?

Conservation Context: Unlike consumer apps where engagement drives success, our success metric was whether stakeholders could extract actionable insights from complex ecological data. This required thoughtful information hierarchy and visual encoding.

Multi-Audience Challenge: The same data needed to be accessible to experts (who want detail) and general public (who need simplified stories). Our design had to support progressive disclosure‚Äîsimple at first glance, detailed on interaction.

Why Prioritize Accessibility Standards?

Inclusive Conservation: Environmental advocacy should be accessible to all community members regardless of ability. Following WCAG guidelines wasn't just good practice‚Äîit aligned with conservation values of inclusivity.

Legal Compliance: Since this tool could potentially be used by council officials, ensuring accessibility compliance protected against potential legal issues while serving the broader community.

Why Interactive Prototyping Over Static Designs?

Complex User Flows: Static screens couldn't capture the dynamic nature of filtering data by time periods, switching between different park views, or drilling down from summary to detail views. Interactive prototypes let stakeholders experience the actual workflow.

Testing Realistic Scenarios: We needed to test whether users could actually complete tasks like "find bat activity changes after festival lighting" rather than just evaluate individual screen designs.

Why Prioritize Mobile-First Design Approach?

Universal Accessibility: Mobile devices are the most accessible to all target audiences‚Äîfrom dedicated botanics volunteers to general public visitors. Unlike specialized equipment or desktop computers, smartphones are universally owned and familiar, removing barriers to adoption across different demographic groups.

On-Site Practicality: Users would realistically access the dashboard while physically in the botanics or parks. Mobile devices allow real-time data checking during evening bat monitoring sessions, immediate reporting of unusual activity, and instant access to species identification while observing bats in their natural habitat.

Immediate Engagement: For public education and "Adopt-a-Bat" features, mobile access enables spontaneous engagement‚Äîvisitors can interact with conservation content immediately upon seeing bats, rather than waiting to access a computer later when interest might wane.

Data Collection Context: Volunteers conducting field work need portable access to historical data for comparison with current observations. Mobile-optimized charts and trend visualizations support evidence-based field decisions and real-time pattern recognition.

Social Sharing Integration: Mobile platforms naturally support sharing conservation content and bat adoption stories on social media, amplifying public awareness and community engagement beyond individual users.

How Site Reconnaissance Shaped Our Design Decisions?

Field-Validated Interface: Direct observation of volunteers using devices in low-light, outdoor conditions confirmed our need for high-contrast visuals, large touch targets, and minimal text input. Real-world usage patterns drove interface simplification.

Connectivity-Aware Features: Discovering spotty cellular coverage in key monitoring areas led us to design offline-capable features, data caching, and clear connectivity status indicators to ensure reliability during field work.

Weather-Responsive Design: Understanding that volunteers work in various weather conditions influenced our decision to use larger buttons, simplified gestures, and voice input options that work even when wearing gloves.

Security-Informed Architecture: Recognizing vandalism risks for equipment led us to prioritize cloud-based data storage with local backup capabilities, ensuring data persistence regardless of hardware issues.

Stage 5: Development & Testing

Building the functional dashboard with user feedback integration

‚è± Duration: 2 Days

üí≠ Strategic Reasoning Behind Our Development & Testing Approach:

Why Web-Based Over Native App Development?

Accessibility Across Devices: Stakeholders use different devices‚Äîvolunteers might use older phones, researchers work on laptops, council officials use tablets in meetings. A responsive web app ensures universal access without requiring app store downloads or device-specific versions.

Deployment Speed: Web deployment means updates reach all users immediately without app store approval processes. Critical for a conservation tool where data freshness and rapid iteration matter more than native performance.

Cross-Platform Consistency: Single codebase means consistent experience across Windows, Mac, iOS, and Android without maintaining separate codebases.

Why Focus on Performance Optimization?

Field Usage Context: Volunteers and researchers might access the dashboard from park locations with limited connectivity. Slow-loading charts or data visualizations could make the tool impractical for field work.

Real-Time Data Requirements: Conservation decisions often need current data‚Äîdelays in loading could mean missing critical ecological events or patterns.

Why User Testing With Multiple Stakeholder Types?

Validation of Multi-User Design: We designed one interface to serve different user types, but this assumption needed validation. Testing with actual volunteers, researchers, and policy-minded users revealed whether our progressive disclosure strategy actually worked.

Real-World Task Validation: Rather than testing isolated features, we tested complete workflows: "Can a volunteer actually find and report unusual bat activity?" "Can a council member find evidence to support a lighting policy decision?"

Iteration Before Launch: Discovering usability issues during testing costs less than discovering them after stakeholders start depending on the tool for actual conservation work.

Why Emphasis on Data Visualization Accuracy?

Scientific Integrity: Misleading charts could lead to incorrect conservation decisions. We prioritized accurate data representation over visually impressive but potentially confusing graphics.

Stakeholder Trust: Researchers and policy makers who spot inaccuracies in data presentation will lose confidence in the entire tool, undermining conservation efforts.

  • Implement interactive data visualizations using Chart.js
  • Conduct usability testing with target users
